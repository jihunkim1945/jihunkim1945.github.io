South Korea, my homeland

Korea is a nation marked by resilience. Emerging from colonization, war, and
rapid modernization, it transformed within a few generations into a global center
of technology and culture. Its strengths lie in education, industriousness, and
a deep sense of community. For many, Korea symbolizes pride, speed, and innovation.
Yet this progress carries weight: intense competition, social pressure, and limited
personal freedoms often leave individuals burdened. The psychology of life in Korea
is a mixture of pride in its achievements and exhaustion from its pace. It is a country
of remarkable energy, but also of quiet struggles beneath the surface.

Flight back to Korea

We trusted the wrong people. Betrayed, we left Canada behind after three long years.
Returning to Korea was not part of the plan—it was only to see my grandmother, who was
gravely ill. We meant to go back to America. But a new president’s policies closed that
path, and we were stranded. My grandmother recovered. She was happy to see us. Perhaps I
should have been glad too.
At first we waited, believing the visa would come. But time passed without change.
My mother enrolled us in a middle school. On the first day, every eye turned toward us. Even in
my own country,I felt the gaze of a stranger.
Was I still an outsider here?
Adapting was hard. The rhythm of Korean life, the weight of its expectations,
pressed on us. Making friends was slow. To them, we were curiosities—foreign
children in a land that should have been ours. I began to wonder if home was only
another place where one learns to endure.
